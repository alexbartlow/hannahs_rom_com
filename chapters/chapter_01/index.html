Chapter 1: The Call

The corgi in Recovery Room B has had a very expensive day and won’t stop screaming about it.

Not literally screaming—veterinary screaming, which is more of a sustained, indignant wheeze that somehow carries the emotional weight of a toddler in a grocery store. I know because I’ve heard both, and the corgi is honestly winning on conviction.

“His gums are perfect,” I say to Priya, who’s pretending not to notice that I’m narrating my own life like someone who spent too long on the internet. “No secondary infection. Dr. Chen’s work is clean.”

“Of course it is.” Priya leans against the counter with the kind of slouch that’s probably bad for her back. She does it anyway because Priya operates under the assumption that good posture is for people with fewer feelings. “You know what’s not clean? The guy I went out with last night. And I don’t mean his apartment, though—oh my God, Nora, there was a pizza box from February under his couch. February.”

I’m still typing up the chart for the corgi, whose name is Sir Reginald, which is either the best or worst thing about him. “Did you go back again after the pizza box incident?”

“No. The pizza box was discovered during the first walkthrough of his living situation. I made an excuse and left.”

“What was the excuse?”

“I said I had to go feed my cat.”

I look up. “You don’t have a cat.”

“He doesn’t know that.”

This is why Priya is my best friend. Not because her dating decisions are good, but because she’s honest about how bad they are. I file that away—the observation, not the lesson—because I don’t actually learn from Priya’s mistakes so much as enjoy them secondhand. My own mistakes are far more serious, which is either a sign of character or a sign that I don’t go on dates, and I’ve stopped trying to figure out which.

“You should do something selfish this weekend,” Priya says, which is what she always says when I’ve been working too hard, which is always.

“I’m off tomorrow,” I say. “That’s pretty selfish.”

“No, that’s baseline. I mean actually selfish. Like, don’t answer your phone for your mom. Don’t return texts immediately. Don’t—”

My phone rings.

It’s an unknown number. I almost don’t answer.

I answer it.

“Hello?” I say, already pulling up my schedule on my other screen.

“Nora? It’s Diane.”

Diane is June’s daughter. June is my neighbor, except not technically—she’s three houses down and still technically a block away, but I’ve absorbed her through proximity and her relentless insistence on being befriended. She brings me novenas. She asks about my dating life like she’s personally invested in its outcomes. Six months ago, she taught me how to make sofrito the way her mother taught her, and now I can’t taste it without thinking of her hands walking me through the recipe.

Diane doesn’t usually call. She texts. This is bad.

“Hi. Is everything okay?”

“No—well, Mom’s in the ambulance right now, but yes, everything’s—look, she fell. In the kitchen. I think it’s her hip. She’s in a lot of pain, and she’s asking for Captain, but she won’t be home for weeks, maybe longer, and I can’t—” Diane’s voice is doing that thing where it starts to fray. “The rehab facility won’t allow pets, and my place doesn’t have the room, and I don’t know what we’re going to do with—”

“Diane. How long has Captain been alone?”

There’s a pause. It’s a very small pause.

“I—since this morning? When she fell? Maybe four hours ago?”

I’m already standing. My bag is under the desk. My keys are in my bag. “Is she in surgery?”

“They’re getting her prepped now.”

“Okay. I’m going to get Captain. Don’t worry about anything except your mom, okay? Captain is handled.”

“Nora, you don’t have to—”

“I do though. It’s fine. I have tomorrow off anyway.”

This is not quite a lie. I do have tomorrow off. What I didn’t mention is that I was planning to spend it exactly as I spend most of my days off: watering plants, responding to work emails, and finding small ways to be useful. Captain is just more honest about it than usual.

I hang up and look at Priya. She’s giving me the face. The I told you to be selfish face.

“June fell. I’m going to grab her dog.”

“Is June okay?”

I’m already halfway out the door.

June’s house is small and perfectly maintained, the way everything in her life is. Bungalow-blue front door. Porch light on a timer. The spare key is in the decorative stone by the planter, exactly where she said it would be six months ago when she insisted I take one, as if she could see into the future and knew exactly when I’d need it.

The moment I open the door, I hear him.

Captain is whimpering from the living room, a sound so bereft it lands in my chest like a stone. I’ve heard dogs in distress before—it’s literally half my job—but this is different. This is someone afraid.

He’s a Great Dane, blue merle, which means he’s enormous and mottled gray in a way that’s supposed to look elegant but right now just looks expensive and sad. He’s pulled one of the throw cushions off the couch and buried himself underneath it, like 140 pounds of dog can fit under a pillow and magically disappear. His whole body is shaking.

I kneel down slowly. This is the voice I use at the clinic, the one that’s steady and low and sounds like a person who’s done this a thousand times before, which I have. “Hey, baby. It’s okay. It’s just me.”

Captain doesn’t emerge from under the cushion.

“You know me. Remember? I was here last week. June gave me coffee. You sat on the porch.”

Still nothing.

I wait. This is the thing people don’t understand about afraid animals. You don’t push them. You sit in the corner of their fear with them until they’re ready to come back out. I’ve got time.

Thirty seconds pass. Then Captain’s nose appears, wet and dark. Then his whole head. Then his body unfolds itself one section at a time until he’s standing, trembling, looking at me like he’s trying to make sure I’m real.

I am. I’m so real. I’m absolutely here.

Captain comes to me in a rush, all momentum and desperation, and tries to fit himself into my lap like he’s still a puppy. He’s not. He’s a 140-pound dog who has no sense of his own size, and his weight knocks me back against the wall. I let it. I can’t move now, pinned by the dead weight of his fear, and I don’t want to.

I check him the way I’d check anything in the clinic. Gums pink. Eyes clear. Breathing elevated but normal. No blood, no injury. He’s just afraid. So afraid that he fell apart waiting for someone who knows his name.

I call Diane back with one hand, the other still on Captain’s trembling spine.

“She’s in recovery,” Diane says. “Everything went well. They’re saying six weeks of physical therapy, then maybe home, but the house isn’t set up for her recovery and my place is too small and—”

“She can come here when she’s ready,” I say. “But for right now, you just focus on her. Captain’s here. I’ve got him.”

“Nora, that’s above and beyond. I can try to find a boarding place—”

“Don’t. I mean it. He’s fine here.”

He will be. I know exactly how to do it.

I stay in June’s living room for the next hour, just sitting with Captain’s weight across my legs, just being the kind of solid and steady that a scared animal needs. I look at her books—the ones stacked on the side table, the ones overflowing from the small bookshelf, the one open face-down on the kitchen counter like June was reading mid-fall. I look at the crossword half-finished on the kitchen table. I look at the photo on the fridge: June and Captain at a neighborhood picnic, June’s head thrown back laughing, Captain wearing a bandana like he’s done something to deserve it.

I touch the photo. June keeps laughing at something I wasn’t there for.

When the light starts going purple outside the windows, I make myself a sandwich from June’s bread and cheese. Captain follows me from room to room like he’s afraid I’ll evaporate if he loses line of sight. I text my mom: June had a fall. I’m staying at her place with the dog. I’m fine.

My mom calls immediately. I could have predicted this. I manage the call the way I manage everything: I reassure, I deflect, I promise to eat something. I hang up before she can ask more questions. This is kindness, in its way.

I set up on the couch with a blanket and my phone. Captain climbs up after me and drapes himself across my legs. He weighs as much as a person. I can’t move. I don’t want to.

Outside, the porch light turns on right on schedule. Captain’s breathing slows. Mine matches it.

I lie in the dark with one hand free, scrolling, and feel the weight of the dog across my legs like an anchor.
