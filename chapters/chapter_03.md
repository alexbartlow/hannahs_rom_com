# Chapter 3: The Negotiation

I wake up to pins and needles and the smell of coffee.

The first part makes sense—Captain has somehow in the night migrated from being across my legs to being *fully* on top of my right leg, all 140 pounds of him, which is an anatomically ambitious position and absolutely cutting off circulation. I extract myself carefully, trying not to wake him, which doesn't work. Captain opens one eye, assesses my crime of leaving, and puts his head back down with a heavy sigh that suggests he's very disappointed in my priorities.

The coffee doesn't make sense.

I didn't make coffee. I don't have the mental energy to make coffee. I have the mental energy to sit on June's couch and stare at a wall and maybe shower at some point, so coffee was definitely not in the plan.

But I smell it, so either June has a very delayed-timer coffee maker (unlikely, she's organized, coffee makers don't work that way), or someone else is in June's house.

This is the part where a smarter person would grab something heavy. I grab my phone instead, which is somehow both better and worse. I follow the smell to the kitchen.

There is a man sitting at June's kitchen table. He's drinking from her "World's Best Retired Teacher" mug with an apple on it, which is June's favorite mug, which means he's either: (a) family, (b) very comfortable here, or (c) has no survival instinct. He's reading something on his phone. He doesn't look like a murderer. He looks like someone who made coffee in someone else's house and decided to sit down while it cooled, which is a level of casual entitlement that either reads as confidence or read as profound obliviousness.

He looks up at me.

We stare at each other.

I am running three simultaneous threat assessments: (1) Is he a threat? (No, he's holding a coffee mug like it's a security blanket.) (2) Who is he? (Unknown.) (3) Do I look okay? (No, I'm in yesterday's scrubs, my hair is in the kind of sleep disaster that requires explanation, and I can feel the couch cushion crease on my cheek like a brand.)

Assessment three annoys me. I don't know this man. Why do I care what I look like?

"Hi," the man says. He stands up, which is what you do when a strange woman appears in your borrowed-house kitchen looking like she's just survived something. "I'm Eli. Kat's brother. Your sister asked me to come check on Captain."

My sister did not ask him to come. Kat is not my sister. But the math works—he said June asked him, not that Kat asked him, and I'm operating on about three hours of sleep and one very dedicated dog, so it takes me a second to track.

"Nora," I say. "I'm... June's neighbor. Friend. I'm also here because June asked me to help with Captain."

Eli processes this. I watch his face go through the exact same realization I'm having: June has double-booked us. Or, more accurately—and this is the kind of thing June would do—she's set up redundant systems. Because June was a teacher and teachers always have a backup plan.

"So," Eli says carefully, "you're the backup dog plan."

"You're the backup dog plan."

"Okay. Do you want coffee?"

This is not what I expected. I expected either defensiveness or confusion. I did not expect casual coffee hospitality in June's kitchen like this is some kind of normal Saturday morning. But I need caffeine to process any of this, so I say yes.

He pours me a cup with the kind of care that suggests he understands that coffee is important. It's good coffee. June does not buy bad coffee.

"So," I say, sitting down across from him at June's table, Captain already settling across both our feet like he's accomplished something, "do you have experience with dogs?"

"Not recent experience, no."

"Large dogs?"

"Had a beagle growing up."

I resist the urge to close my eyes. A beagle is not a large dog. A beagle is a medium dog with opinions. Captain is a large dog with existential anxiety. These are not the same category.

"Do you know his routine?" I ask, pulling up Captain's vet records on my phone because I need to at least pretend I have some control over this situation.

"I just got here," he says, which is honest and somehow more annoying than a lie would have been.

I start talking. It comes out as a list. The signs of stress: panting, pacing, tucking his tail. The de-escalation: calm voice, low movement, staying in the room. The things to avoid: the toaster (sounds like an emergency), plastic bags (approximately as dangerous as knives), the UPS truck (an actual threat, apparently, or at least Captain treats it like one).

Eli listens. He doesn't interrupt. He doesn't take notes. He just listens the way someone does when they're trying to catalog information without acting like you're giving a presentation.

When I finally stop talking, he says, "You're very good at making sure everyone knows exactly what to do."

My body tenses.

But Eli just says: "He's lucky to have you."

I look at Captain. Still asleep across our feet.

"I need to go home," I say. "Shower. Check in with Diane. Can you handle him for the next few hours?"

"I'll try," Eli says, which is at least honest.

I walk him through the practical: where the food is, how much Captain eats, where his leash is, the backyard gate that sticks on the left side, the squeaky taco that is apparently his most prized possession. "Do not lose the squeaky taco," I say, and I'm not joking.

"I won't lose the dog's taco," Eli repeats solemnly.

Captain, sensing that I'm leaving, surfaces from whatever dog dreams he's been having and follows me to the door. He's not dramatic about it—no crying, no blocking my exit—but he presses his whole head into my neck like he can absorb my presence through osmosis. I kneel down, which puts me at eye level with a very large sad dog.

"You're okay, big guy," I say quietly, the voice I use at the clinic, the one that means *I've got this, you're safe*. "I'll be back tonight. Eli's going to hang out with you."

I look up at Eli, who's standing by the table like he's not sure if he's allowed to be in this moment.

"He'll probably test you," I say. "Dogs do that. He'll wait to see if you'll stay when he gets scared."

Eli looks at Captain. At me. At Captain again.

"Don't we all," he says quietly.

I blink.

I stand up, tell Eli to text if anything comes up, and leave.

In the car, I think about the man in June's kitchen. Not what I expected. He made coffee like it was normal. He listened without looking bored. He said *don't we all* like he meant something I didn't ask.

I don't know him. I just need him to not lose the squeaky taco.

I drive home and don't think about the rest.
