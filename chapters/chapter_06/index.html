Chapter 6: The Notebook

The days have shape now.

Wake at 7, shower, walk to June’s. Three doors down, two blocks over. Close enough that I could do it in my sleep, far enough that it feels like going somewhere. The walk matters. It separates Kat’s house (where I sleep, where I fail to write, where my sister looks at me like an X-ray) from June’s house (where Captain waits, where there’s a routine, where I have a reason to exist).

Coffee at June’s now — my own coffee. Not June’s anymore. That felt like crossing a line. Now there’s a bag of beans I bought, a mug becoming mine, a routine of making it while Captain watches from his spot by the window.

Feed, walk, settle. By mid-morning, Captain is back on the couch or the kitchen floor. I read aloud — articles, poems, the Billy Collins I found on June’s shelf. Captain’s breathing aligns with my voice. Steady. Timed. A metronome with fur.

The notebook came back two weeks in.

It’s been at Kat’s on my nightstand since I started this job. Empty since June’s fall. But one morning last week, I found myself with it and no good reason not to open it, so I did.

The haikus started as a joke. Something to do while Captain napped and the house was quiet and my brain was doing laps around the blank Google Doc that still existed, still mocked me, still represented every job I couldn’t land and every sentence I couldn’t write. So I wrote haikus. Deliberately bad ones. Syllable counts slightly off on purpose. Observations that reached for profundity and landed on something just slightly wrong.

Mail carrier ran / Captain just stood there, confused / Who’s scared of whom now

June’s garden needs help / I don’t know flowers from weeds / Neither does the dog

They were about Captain, mostly. The dog became a reliable subject — his fears, his quirks, his inexplicable loyalty to a squeaky toy shaped like a taco. The haikus were bad, I knew they were bad, and this freed me to write them. No stakes. No audience. No one would ever read them, which meant I could fail silently, endlessly, without the failure mattering.

By day eight, I stopped writing only about Captain.

I wrote about the mail carrier who comes up the driveway and gets stopped short by a 140-pound dog in the window. About the sound of Kat’s car pulling into the driveway back at home. About June’s garden going to seed because there’s no one to tend it. About the light in June’s living room at 6 PM when everything goes golden and temporary.

About the way Nora moves when she’s tired from work. The way she kneels down to the dog. The way she says “Hey, baby” in a voice that has nothing to do with performing anything.

This is the problem.

The evening handoff arrives like a tradition. 5:30 PM. Nora’s car pulls in. Captain’s tail beats an immediate hello. I do the verbal debrief — Captain ate, walked, no incidents, the toaster situation remains unresolved. We talk for a few minutes. Then Nora kneels to the dog and I notice, with the precision of someone who has trained himself to notice things, that she smells like the clinic. Antiseptic. Sharp and clean.

And under that: something else. Something like lavender. Something that has nothing to do with the clinic and everything to do with the fact that I’m now paying attention to Nora in a way that wasn’t part of the agreement.

This is a professional dog co-parenting arrangement.

I don’t say it out loud. I think it, loud, like a prayer or a curse.

Nora doesn’t stay long. She’s tired. Early shift tomorrow. She takes Captain home — or rather, Captain takes her. They have their own rhythm now, separate from me. I stay at June’s, straighten the kitchen, walk home, and try not to think about the fact that I’m now in the category of person who notices what someone smells like and treats it like a crisis.

Kat is eating standing up in her kitchen when I get back. This is how surgeons eat, apparently. Standing up, one-handed, in scrubs, like they might have to leave at any moment.

“So,” I say, too casually, “how well do you know Nora?”

Kat looks at me the way she looks at X-rays. With complete clarity and zero mercy.

“Why?” she asks.

“Just asking. She seems… she’s good with the dog.”

“She’s a vet tech. That’s literally her job.”

“Right. Yeah. That’s what I meant.”

Kat studies me. The way she studied me when I lost the job, the way she studies anyone who doesn’t want to be studied.

“This is a professional dog co-parenting arrangement,” I say.

“Nobody said it wasn’t,” Kat replies, which is the most dangerous sentence she could possibly say.

I don’t sleep well. In the morning, the notebook is on the desk. I flip through. Read the ones about Captain. Read the ones about the neighborhood. Read the ones about the light.

Then I open a new page.

She smells like two things. One is clean, one is a flower. This is about dog.
