
## Chapter 2 — ELI | "The Pitch"
**Word target:** ~1,600 words
**Purpose:** Establish Eli's voice, his unemployment shame spiral, his relationship with Kat, and the moment he meets Nora (asleep, from his perspective). First haiku.

### Scene 1: Kat's Kitchen Table (≈500 words)
- **Setting:** Friday morning. A nice kitchen in a nice house that belongs to Eli's sister and her husband. Eli is at the table with a laptop open to a blank Google Doc that's been blank for forty-five minutes. A mug of coffee has gone cold. The cursor blinks.
- **Action:** Eli is supposed to be working on a freelance pitch for a pet food brand's social campaign. He has notes. He has a brief. He has nothing. The part of his brain that used to be good at this — the part that could spin a headline in ten minutes — has been offline for months. He writes a sentence. Deletes it. Writes half of another. Minimizes the doc. Opens LinkedIn. Closes LinkedIn immediately because LinkedIn is a psychological weapon.
- **Character beats:** Eli's internal voice is dry, self-aware, and quietly vicious toward himself. He's funny about it — that's the defense mechanism. He narrates his own failure with the same precision he used to bring to copy. "I am a man who once wrote a tagline that sold eleven million dollars of oat milk, and I cannot currently string together a sentence about kibble."
- **He hears Kat** on the phone in the other room. Catches fragments: "...broke her hip... surgery went fine... someone needs to... the dog..."
- **Plant:** On the table next to his laptop is a Moleskine notebook. It's closed. He hasn't written in it in two weeks. The last entry, if we could see it, is a haiku about his sister's kitchen ceiling.

### Scene 2: The Pitch (≈400 words)
- **Action:** Kat comes in. She tells him about June — the neighbor three doors down, retired teacher, nice woman, giant dog. Hip fracture, surgery, rehab for weeks. The dog can't be alone. Kat would do it but she's on a surgical rotation and Dayo travels for work.
- **She looks at Eli.** Not pointedly. (Very pointedly.)
- **Eli resists.** He doesn't know June well. He's not a dog person. He has work to do. (He gestures at the blank Google Doc.) Kat, who is a surgeon and therefore comfortable with direct incisions: "Eli. You need a reason to leave this house that isn't a LinkedIn rabbit hole. The dog needs someone calm. You are, despite all evidence in your facial expression right now, a calm person."
- **Character beats:** The sibling dynamic is warm but has an edge — Kat loves him, worries about him, and is not remotely careful about his feelings. Eli loves her back and resents that she's right about almost everything. He agrees to go check on the dog. Not commit. Just check.
- **Transition:** He closes the laptop (the Doc is still blank). Pockets his phone. Leaves the notebook on the table. Walks three houses down.

### Scene 3: June's House (≈500 words)
- **Setting:** June's bungalow. The door is unlocked (Nora forgot to re-lock it). Eli knocks. No answer. He texts Kat: "Nobody's here. The door's open. Do I just... go in?" Kat: "You're not robbing the place. Go check on the dog."
- **Action:** He steps inside. The house is quiet. Books everywhere. The crossword on the table. He can hear breathing — deep, slow, not-a-dog breathing. He follows the sound to the living room.
- **The image:** Nora is asleep on the couch. Captain is draped across her legs, also asleep. She's in scrubs — she never changed out of them. Her phone is on her chest, still open to a text thread. Captain's head is hanging off the edge of the couch at an angle that cannot be comfortable but clearly is.
- **Eli's reaction:** He doesn't know who this woman is. He should probably leave. He should definitely not be standing in a stranger's living room watching a stranger sleep. But the dog opens one eye, looks at him, assesses him, and puts his head back down. Not a threat, apparently. Captain has vetted him and found him acceptable.
- **He retreats to the kitchen.** Decides making coffee is a reasonable thing a reasonable person would do while waiting for the stranger on the couch to wake up. He finds June's coffee. He makes a pot. He sits at June's kitchen table — much like Kat's, except this one has a crossword instead of a blank Google Doc, which is somehow less depressing.
- **Character beats:** Eli notices things. The books (June's taste runs to mysteries and poetry — he clocks the Billy Collins). The photo on the fridge (same one Nora noticed). The way the sleeping woman's hand is resting on the dog's back, fingers slightly curled, like she fell asleep mid-stroke. He notices and catalogs and does nothing with the information, which is his whole deal.

### Scene 4: The Haiku (≈200 words)
- **Closing beat:** Eli sitting at June's kitchen table, drinking coffee that belongs to a woman he's never met, in a house that isn't his, waiting for a stranger to wake up so he can explain why he's there. The coffee is good. The house is quiet. Captain is snoring.
- **He pulls out his phone** and opens his notes app (the notebook is at Kat's but the habit travels). Types:

> *Dog on a stranger*
> *I came here to help someone*
> *The couch is taken*

- **Last line territory:** He reads it back. It's not good. He saves it anyway. That's the thing about writing — even bad, even pointless, even when no one will ever read it. He can't stop.
