# Chapter 5: The Routine

By Wednesday of the second week, the mornings have a rhythm.

I'm at June's by 7 AM, keys in the decorative stone, coffee already made because Eli apparently wakes up early and doesn't know what to do with the quiet. Captain hears my car and is waiting by the back door, tail going but controlled. He's better on walks now — still nervous about plastic bags and leaf-blowers and the general chaos of the world outside, but functional. We loop the block. He sniffs things. Sometimes he pulls toward something interesting. It's almost normal.

The notes started appearing by day three.

First, Eli left one by the coffee maker: *Did dishes. Fed Captain. He stared at the toaster for eleven minutes today. I think he's planning something.*

I texted back: *Captain cannot operate a toaster. You're safe.*

By Friday, he'd left: *Sat with Captain on the porch. He seems to like it better when you're not there to judge his choices.*

Today there's a Post-it on the kitchen counter: *Captain learned to high-five. Approximately. He mostly just makes a slapping noise and looks confused. I'll teach him the proper form before you catch me.*

The texts during the day started as practical. *Fed at noon, no incidents.* *Walked at 2, good temperament.* By the end of the first week, he was sending photos.

Yesterday: Captain asleep on the couch with the squeaky taco tucked under his chin like a security blanket, captioned *He's guarding it with his life. I respect his priorities.*

I showed it to Priya at the clinic. She laughed — actually laughed — and said, "Oh no. He's funny."

"It's not like that," I said.

"Okay," she said, the way people say okay when they mean *sure*.

The evening handoffs have become the part of my day I think about while I'm at work. Not in a way I've examined. Just that when I'm driving to June's, the traffic doesn't feel like traffic. It feels like I'm going toward something.

Today Eli's already on the porch when I pull up. He's sitting on the top step with Captain beside him, and the light is doing that thing it does in late afternoon — turning everything gold, making everything feel temporary and important at the same time. He's reading something on his phone and doesn't notice me at first. He just looks... settled. Different from the man I found in June's kitchen two weeks ago, who was coiled tight like he was waiting for something to break.

"Good day?" I ask, and he looks up like he's been pulled from somewhere far away.

"Yeah. No incidents. He ate well. Captain's good."

This is what we usually do. The debrief. Then I take over and Eli heads back to Kat's and the day is segmented neatly into before-Eli and after-Eli, which is a thing I'm not thinking about.

But today Eli says, "It's nice out. I'm not in a rush if you're not."

I sit down on the step next to him.

"So you're a copywriter," I say, because that's where we are now — somewhere after logistics, somewhere that includes questions. "What does that actually involve?"

"Ads, mostly. Headlines, slogans, the things that make people buy things they don't need by making them feel something about it." He talks with his hands when he's interested. His hands are moving now. "I was at an agency for eight years. Laid off six months ago, so now I'm freelancing, which is to say I'm writing pitches that mostly don't land but occasionally one does and I remember why I liked it."

This is the part where he smooths over the edges. Freelancing. The word sits fine. I have no reason to doubt it.

"You must be good at it," I say. "You make dog updates sound like they're about something."

"That's the secret of advertising. Everything is about something if you frame it right." He looks at Captain. "Though in his case, it's just about him. The staring, the fear, the squeaky taco that he treats like it's the Holy Grail. What you see is what you get."

"That's not true," I say. "He's gotten better since you've been here."

"He's gotten calmer. That's just because I'm incompetent in a very non-threatening way."

I tell him about the clinic. Not the competent version I usually present, but the actual version — where I admit that some days are hard, where owners don't deserve the animals they have, where I come home feeling like I've absorbed someone else's entire emotional weight and can't quite put it down. He doesn't try to fix it. He just listens and responds like it matters, which is rarer than it should be.

"That's a lot," he says.

"It's just the job."

"No," he says. "I mean, you take a lot on. You notice that?"

I don't know how to answer that, so I don't.

We talk about June. He has a story about calling her from the backyard when Captain refused to go outside because of the plastic bag, and she told him that Captain once ate her rain boots and had apparently decided plastic was a legitimate threat. I have a story about June teaching me how to make sofrito and spending the whole time talking about Eli like she'd never met him but had very strong opinions on how he was doing. We laugh.

"She's meddlesome," I say.

"She's testing things," Eli says. "I think she knew exactly what she was doing when she set this up."

"You mean co-fostering?"

"I mean asking us both."

The evening is getting darker. There's a quality to the light now that means I should probably go — I have an early shift, and I've been here for over an hour, and my phone has gone unchecked for forty minutes, which never happens. But the step is warm and Captain is between us, completely settled, his breathing even and calm. He's not anxious. He's not testing anyone. He's just here, the way the three of us are just here.

"I should go," I say, and I don't move for another minute.

When I stand up, Captain lifts his head like he's checking whether this is okay. Eli says something to him — quiet, the same voice I use at the clinic — and Captain puts his head back down.

In the car, no music, just thinking. The easy way the conversation moved. The way he asked real questions instead of polite ones.

I get home and check my phone. Eli texted two minutes ago: *Thanks for staying. Captain's still asking where you went.*

I text back: *Tell him I'll be back tomorrow.*

Then I stand in my kitchen for a second, holding the phone, feeling the warmth of the evening the way you feel the warmth of sun on your skin after it goes away.

It stays with me.
