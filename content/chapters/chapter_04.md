# Chapter 4: The Handoff

The sticky note is in Nora's handwriting. Precise. Urgent in the way that only someone very organized can be urgent.

*Captain: 2 cups kibble, 1 PM sharp. Fresh water. Afternoon walk after meal (wait 15 min). If he won't go outside due to WIND or BAGS, sit with him in the yard for 5 minutes, then try again. He's testing. Don't give in but don't force. Text me if anything.*

Below that: her number, which I already have, added to my phone as "Nora (Captain)" — a label that feels like a definition of how my life has reorganized itself in the last sixteen hours.

I read the note twice. The coffee is already made. The house is quiet except for Captain, who's breathing under the kitchen table with the weight of certainty.

I sit down and open my laptop.

The Google Doc is still blank. The cursor blinks like an accusation. I type one sentence: *Feed your dog like you love the planet.*

I delete it.

I open another: *Sustainability isn't a luxury.*

Delete that too.

I close the laptop.

Captain shifts under the table. His massive head settles directly across my feet. Not moving. Just lying there. Making it impossible for me to stand without disturbing him, which I don't do.

I sit. The weight of the dog on my feet is warm and absolute.

Around 10 AM, Captain finds a shoe. One of June's garden clogs, left by the back door. He approaches it like it's a puzzle, nose down, methodical. By the time I notice the sound — soft, rhythmic destruction — it's too late. The clog is in three pieces.

I hold up the remains. Captain looks at me with the expression of someone who has completed a task and expects no commentary.

"Okay," I say. "That's... that's fine. We just destroyed June's shoe. That's fine."

Captain lies down.

At 11:30, I decide to take the dog outside. Nora said he needs an afternoon walk. I clip the leash — Captain accepts with the resignation of someone who knows what's coming — and we head toward the back door.

Captain makes it three feet into the yard before he sees it.

A plastic bag. Caught on the fence. Fluttering like a flag.

Captain stops. His body gets very low. His tail disappears entirely underneath him.

"It's just a bag," I say. "It's sitting on a fence. It's not even moving very much."

The bag flutters.

Captain does not move.

I try treats. Nora left treats — organized in little containers with labels. Captain ignores them. I try gently pulling the leash. Captain becomes a 140-pound unmovable object. I try calling June to see if there's some magic word, some incantation, but June is in physical therapy and I'm just standing in her backyard with a terrified dog and a plastic bag that will not stop existing.

I give up. We go back inside.

By 2 PM, my laptop is still blank, and Captain has discovered that sitting on a laptop while it's open is the best possible activity. I extract the computer with Captain's full 140-pound cooperation — the dog is dead weight, a furry boulder with opinions — and the laptop makes a sound it probably shouldn't make.

The Google Doc is still blank.

"At least it's consistent," I say to Captain, who doesn't care.

I call June. I expect a frail voice, maybe some gentle authority. I get a woman with the vocal authority of someone who spent forty years reading teenagers.

"The shoe situation," I say. "I wanted to apologize."

"Darling, he ate my rain boots last spring," June says. "The clog is a modest sacrifice. How is he otherwise?"

"There's a plastic bag on your fence."

"Ah yes. He saw one blow across the yard when he was a puppy and has never recovered. We all have our traumas. Does he seem otherwise okay?"

"He won't go outside. He destroyed property. He sat on my computer."

"Yes," June says, like this is all completely reasonable. "I understand those are concerns. Tell me — how are *you* doing?"

I don't know how to answer that. The question is too broad. It contains too many small failures.

"I don't really know what I'm doing," I say instead.

There's a pause. June shifts positions, maybe sitting up.

"Eli, dear. He doesn't need someone who knows what they're doing. He needs someone calm. You're very calm."

I'm not feeling calm. I'm feeling like a man in someone else's kitchen with a dog I don't understand and a blank Google Doc and a sister who sent me here as charity work. But June says it with the authority of someone who spent decades reading people, and I don't argue.

"Okay," I say.

"Good. Give him time. He'll settle. And for God's sake, don't let him eat my other shoe."

I hang up.

I sit on the floor of June's living room. Not the couch — the floor. Back against the sofa, legs stretched out in front of me. I don't have a plan. I just need to exist somewhere that isn't standing or failing at something.

Captain comes to me slowly. Sniffs my shoulder. Circles once, twice. Then lies down against my side, pressing the full length of himself against me like he's trying to become part of the wall.

I pull out my phone. I open a random article — something about ocean currents, I don't really register — and start reading it aloud. Not performing. Just... reading. My voice quiet, even. Filling the silence with words that aren't mine, so I don't have to think about the words that are.

Captain's breathing slows.

The trembling that's been underneath the dog all day stops.

Captain's head drops onto my thigh, and his whole body becomes completely still.

I keep reading. Ocean currents I don't care about, in a voice that sounds like someone who has figured something out, except I haven't figured anything out. I'm just sitting here. I'm just being still.

It's maybe the most useful thing I've done in six months.

When Nora's car pulls up around 6 PM, Captain doesn't move from my side. His tail beats the floor from his sprawl position, but he doesn't get up. He just stays there, taking stock.

Nora comes in with her keys already in her hand and stops.

"How was he?" she asks.

"He destroyed a shoe. He won't go outside because there's a bag. He sat on my laptop. He's fine."

Nora almost smiles. "He tested you."

"I failed most of the tests."

"He's still here. You passed the only one that matters."

I look at Captain. Captain looks at Nora. Nobody moves.

Later, alone at Kat's, I open my notes app and type:

*Giant coward dog*
*You and me, afraid of bags*
*At least you're honest*
