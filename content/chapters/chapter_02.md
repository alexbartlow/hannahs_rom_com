# Chapter 2: The Pitch

The blank Google Doc has been blank for forty-five minutes, which is a personal record. I've stared at blank pages before—plenty of them, increasingly, over the last six months—but there was usually at least the ghost of an idea somewhere in the white space. A headline hovering like a half-remembered dream. A phrase that might become something if I let it sit long enough.

This time there's nothing. The cursor blinks at me like an accusation.

"Write something about the benefits of sustainable sourcing," the brief said. "Make it feel authentic. Make them feel like they're choosing the planet when they choose our kibble."

I wrote one sentence: *Feed your dog like you love the planet.*

Then I deleted it.

I wrote another: *Sustainability isn't a luxury. It's a responsibility.*

Deleted that too.

I wrote: *Even kibble can change the world.*

I stared at that one for a full minute before closing the tab entirely and opening LinkedIn, which was a mistake. LinkedIn is a psychological weapon designed by people who have clearly never lost a job and are therefore unaware that the scrolling algorithm functions like a very specific kind of torture. *Your network is growing.* *Check out these job opportunities.* *15 people viewed your profile.* Not one of them apparently willing to hire me.

I closed LinkedIn. I opened a blank doc. I stared at the cursor.

The coffee has gone cold.

I stare at the cursor.

I hear Kat on the phone in the other room. She's using her surgeon voice—calm, precise. *...broke her hip...* *...surgery went well...* *...someone needs to...* *...the dog...*

Kat comes in. Still in her scrubs.

"June broke her hip," Kat says. She sits down across from me like this is a casual conversation we're having, not an interruption of my failure.

"Okay," I say. "Is she—"

"She'll be fine. Surgery went well. She's going to need rehab, which means weeks away from home. Which means her dog—this enormous, anxious Great Dane—is going to be alone in her house for six weeks, and she has no one to take care of him."

I don't say anything.

"Dayo's in Denver," Kat continues, "and I'm on a surgical rotation until next week. Mom's two hours away. You're three houses down and you're not doing anything."

"I'm working," I say, gesturing at the blank Google Doc.

Kat looks at the blank Google Doc. She looks at me. She doesn't have to say anything. She's a surgeon. She knows how to get right to the point.

"Eli. You need a reason to leave this house that isn't LinkedIn. The dog needs someone calm. You are, despite all evidence of your current facial expression, actually a calm person."

"I'm not a dog person."

"You were three years ago. You dogsit for your neighbors for six months. You were fine."

"That was a golden retriever who was already happy. Captain—" I don't know how I know June has a dog named Captain. I've never met June. Kat must have mentioned it a hundred times and I stored it in the useless part of my brain that remembers things I don't want to know. "—is anxious."

"Exactly." Kat stands up. "Go check on him. Go sit with him for an hour. Make yourself useful instead of whatever this is." She gestures at the blank screen with a surgeon's disdain for things that aren't actually problems but are pretending to be.

"I'm working on a pitch."

"Eli. The pitch will still be there in an hour. The dog probably won't be okay in an hour, but the dog will still be there. Go."

I close the laptop. The Doc is still blank.

"Take the keys," Kat calls behind me. "Use the front door like a human being."

I already know where June lives.

---

June's house is smaller than Kat's, painted that specific blue-gray that's supposed to be soothing but mostly reads as "New England cottage core." I knock on the door. No one answers. I knock again.

I text Kat: *Nobody's here. Door's open. Do I just... go in?*

Kat: *You're not robbing the place. Go check on the dog.*

The door is unlocked. I step inside.

There are books everywhere.

I follow the sound of breathing that isn't quite a dog sound—deeper than a dog, slower—to the living room.

There's a woman on the couch. She's asleep, completely unconscious, her phone still open on her chest mid-text. She's wearing scrubs, which means she's come from somewhere that requires scrubs. A hospital, probably. Or a vet clinic. She has a dog's warmth under her hand.

Captain—and I'm pretty sure this is Captain, because he's enormous and blue and merle-colored and looks like the kind of dog that would destroy a house if left alone—has his head hanging off the edge of the couch at an anatomically impossible angle. His breathing is deep and even. He's asleep too.

I should leave. This is genuinely a situation where the polite thing to do is leave.

But then Captain's eye cracks open. He looks at me. He assesses me the way dogs do—a full two-second evaluation of whether I'm a threat. Then he puts his head back down and goes back to sleep.

I am apparently vetted.

I go to the kitchen and make coffee. The smell fills the quiet house.

I sit at June's kitchen table with the mug in both hands.

I pull out my phone and open the notes app. I type:

*Dog on a stranger*
*I came here to help someone*
*The couch is taken*

I save it.

The coffee is good. The house is quiet. I wait for one of them to wake up.
