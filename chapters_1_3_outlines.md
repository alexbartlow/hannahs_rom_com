# The Worst Haiku You've Ever Heard
## Detailed Chapter Outlines: Chapters 1–3

---

## Chapter 1 — NORA | "The Call"
**Word target:** ~1,600 words
**Purpose:** Establish Nora's voice, her relationship with June, her competence with animals, and the subtle way she uses caretaking as armor.

### Scene 1: The Clinic (≈400 words)
- **Setting:** Small-animal veterinary clinic, end of a Thursday shift. Fluorescent lights, the smell of surgical scrub, a corgi in recovery from a dental cleaning.
- **Action:** Nora is finishing up charts. Her coworker **Priya** (vet tech, best friend, zero filter) is telling her about a date that went sideways. Nora is laughing, engaged, the version of herself she likes best — competent, social, needed.
- **Phone rings.** It's an unknown number. Nora almost doesn't answer.
- **Character beats:** Nora's internal voice is warm but slightly performative — she narrates herself the way someone does when they're used to being watched. Quick, funny observations. She notices everything about other people. Almost nothing about her own emotional state.
- **Plant:** Priya says something like "You're off tomorrow, right? Do something selfish for once." Nora laughs it off.

### Scene 2: The Call (≈350 words)
- **Action:** It's **Diane**, June's daughter, calling from the hospital. June fell in her kitchen, broken hip, going into surgery. Diane is across town and can't get to June's house — can Nora check on Captain?
- **Nora's reaction:** Immediate pivot to action mode. No hesitation. She's already grabbing her keys while asking questions. What time did June fall? How long has Captain been alone? Has he been fed?
- **Character beats:** Nora's anxiety shows up as logistics. She doesn't say "Oh no, is June okay?" first — she says "How long has Captain been by himself?" Then catches herself and asks about June. This is who she is: she takes care of the thing in front of her. The worry comes later, privately.
- **Transition:** Nora tells Priya she has to go. Priya: "What happened?" Nora, already halfway out the door: "June fell. The dog's alone." Priya: "Is June okay?" Nora, keys in hand, pausing: "...I need to find out."

### Scene 3: June's House (≈500 words)
- **Setting:** A small, tidy bungalow. Porch light on a timer. The front door is locked — Nora has a spare key (of course she does). The moment she opens it, she can hear Captain whimpering from the living room.
- **Action:** The house is mostly fine, but Captain has pulled one cushion off the couch and is lying on it in the corner, shaking. He's a blue merle Great Dane, enormous, and he's made himself as small as possible. There's an untouched bowl of kibble in the kitchen. Nora kneels down, talks to him in the low steady voice she uses at the clinic. Captain comes to her slowly, then all at once — 140 pounds of dog trying to fit in her lap.
- **Character beats:** This is Nora at her most authentic. No performance, no audience. Just her and a scared animal and the thing she's genuinely good at. She checks him over — gums, eyes, breathing. He's not hurt. He's just afraid.
- **She calls Diane** for an update. June is out of surgery, stable, will be in rehab for weeks. Diane says: "I don't know what we're going to do about Captain. Mom's place doesn't allow—" Nora cuts her off: "I've got him. Don't worry about Captain."
- **Plant:** Nora looks around June's house. Books everywhere. A crossword half-finished on the kitchen table. A photo on the fridge of June and Captain at some kind of neighborhood picnic — June is laughing, Captain is wearing a bandana. Nora touches the photo. She doesn't let herself feel the worry yet. She has a dog to take care of.

### Scene 4: The Night (≈350 words)
- **Action:** Nora settles in. She texts her mom ("June had a fall, I'm staying at her place with the dog, I'm fine"). Alma immediately calls. Nora manages the call — reassures, deflects concern about herself, promises to eat something. Hangs up.
- **She makes herself a sandwich** from June's kitchen (June would insist). Captain follows her from room to room, pressing against her legs. She sets up on the couch with a blanket. Captain climbs up — all of him — and drapes himself across her legs. He weighs as much as a person. Nora can't move. She doesn't want to.
- **Closing beat:** Nora lying on June's couch in the dark, pinned by a dog, scrolling her phone with one hand. She should be uncomfortable. She should go home. She has the day off tomorrow and nothing planned — Priya told her to do something selfish. This is the opposite of selfish. She stays anyway. Captain's breathing slows. Hers matches it.
- **Last line territory:** Something like: "This is the thing about me that I can't fix. Someone needs something, and I'm already there. I don't know how to not be already there."

## Chapter 2 — ELI | "The Pitch"
**Word target:** ~1,600 words
**Purpose:** Establish Eli's voice, his unemployment shame spiral, his relationship with Kat, and the moment he meets Nora (asleep, from his perspective). First haiku.

### Scene 1: Kat's Kitchen Table (≈500 words)
- **Setting:** Friday morning. A nice kitchen in a nice house that belongs to Eli's sister and her husband. Eli is at the table with a laptop open to a blank Google Doc that's been blank for forty-five minutes. A mug of coffee has gone cold. The cursor blinks.
- **Action:** Eli is supposed to be working on a freelance pitch for a pet food brand's social campaign. He has notes. He has a brief. He has nothing. The part of his brain that used to be good at this — the part that could spin a headline in ten minutes — has been offline for months. He writes a sentence. Deletes it. Writes half of another. Minimizes the doc. Opens LinkedIn. Closes LinkedIn immediately because LinkedIn is a psychological weapon.
- **Character beats:** Eli's internal voice is dry, self-aware, and quietly vicious toward himself. He's funny about it — that's the defense mechanism. He narrates his own failure with the same precision he used to bring to copy. "I am a man who once wrote a tagline that sold eleven million dollars of oat milk, and I cannot currently string together a sentence about kibble."
- **He hears Kat** on the phone in the other room. Catches fragments: "...broke her hip... surgery went fine... someone needs to... the dog..."
- **Plant:** On the table next to his laptop is a Moleskine notebook. It's closed. He hasn't written in it in two weeks. The last entry, if we could see it, is a haiku about his sister's kitchen ceiling.

### Scene 2: The Pitch (≈400 words)
- **Action:** Kat comes in. She tells him about June — the neighbor three doors down, retired teacher, nice woman, giant dog. Hip fracture, surgery, rehab for weeks. The dog can't be alone. Kat would do it but she's on a surgical rotation and Dayo travels for work.
- **She looks at Eli.** Not pointedly. (Very pointedly.)
- **Eli resists.** He doesn't know June well. He's not a dog person. He has work to do. (He gestures at the blank Google Doc.) Kat, who is a surgeon and therefore comfortable with direct incisions: "Eli. You need a reason to leave this house that isn't a LinkedIn rabbit hole. The dog needs someone calm. You are, despite all evidence in your facial expression right now, a calm person."
- **Character beats:** The sibling dynamic is warm but has an edge — Kat loves him, worries about him, and is not remotely careful about his feelings. Eli loves her back and resents that she's right about almost everything. He agrees to go check on the dog. Not commit. Just check.
- **Transition:** He closes the laptop (the Doc is still blank). Pockets his phone. Leaves the notebook on the table. Walks three houses down.

### Scene 3: June's House (≈500 words)
- **Setting:** June's bungalow. The door is unlocked (Nora forgot to re-lock it). Eli knocks. No answer. He texts Kat: "Nobody's here. The door's open. Do I just... go in?" Kat: "You're not robbing the place. Go check on the dog."
- **Action:** He steps inside. The house is quiet. Books everywhere. The crossword on the table. He can hear breathing — deep, slow, not-a-dog breathing. He follows the sound to the living room.
- **The image:** Nora is asleep on the couch. Captain is draped across her legs, also asleep. She's in scrubs — she never changed out of them. Her phone is on her chest, still open to a text thread. Captain's head is hanging off the edge of the couch at an angle that cannot be comfortable but clearly is.
- **Eli's reaction:** He doesn't know who this woman is. He should probably leave. He should definitely not be standing in a stranger's living room watching a stranger sleep. But the dog opens one eye, looks at him, assesses him, and puts his head back down. Not a threat, apparently. Captain has vetted him and found him acceptable.
- **He retreats to the kitchen.** Decides making coffee is a reasonable thing a reasonable person would do while waiting for the stranger on the couch to wake up. He finds June's coffee. He makes a pot. He sits at June's kitchen table — much like Kat's, except this one has a crossword instead of a blank Google Doc, which is somehow less depressing.
- **Character beats:** Eli notices things. The books (June's taste runs to mysteries and poetry — he clocks the Billy Collins). The photo on the fridge (same one Nora noticed). The way the sleeping woman's hand is resting on the dog's back, fingers slightly curled, like she fell asleep mid-stroke. He notices and catalogs and does nothing with the information, which is his whole deal.

### Scene 4: The Haiku (≈200 words)
- **Closing beat:** Eli sitting at June's kitchen table, drinking coffee that belongs to a woman he's never met, in a house that isn't his, waiting for a stranger to wake up so he can explain why he's there. The coffee is good. The house is quiet. Captain is snoring.
- **He pulls out his phone** and opens his notes app (the notebook is at Kat's but the habit travels). Types:

> *Dog on a stranger*
> *I came here to help someone*
> *The couch is taken*

- **Last line territory:** He reads it back. It's not good. He saves it anyway. That's the thing about writing — even bad, even pointless, even when no one will ever read it. He can't stop.

## Chapter 3 — NORA | "The Negotiation"
**Word target:** ~1,600 words
**Purpose:** The first real interaction. Establish the dynamic — Nora's competence vs. Eli's detachment, and the friction that comes from two people who are both performing slightly less authentic versions of themselves.

### Scene 1: Waking Up (≈350 words)
- **Setting:** June's living room. Nora surfaces slowly. Captain has shifted in the night and is now lying fully on top of her right leg, which is asleep. Pins and needles. She smells coffee, which is confusing because she didn't make coffee.
- **Action:** She extracts herself from under Captain (a process), stands up, and follows the coffee smell to the kitchen. There is a man she has never seen before sitting at June's table, drinking from June's "World's Best Retired Teacher" mug, reading something on his phone.
- **Beat:** They stare at each other.
- **Nora's internal reaction:** Three simultaneous processes: (1) threat assessment (he's not threatening — he's holding a coffee mug with a apple on it), (2) social calibration (is this Diane's husband? a neighbor?), (3) self-consciousness (she's in yesterday's scrubs, her hair is a disaster, she has a crease on her face from the couch cushion). Process (3) annoys her. She shouldn't care what she looks like. She doesn't know this man.

### Scene 2: The Introduction (≈400 words)
- **Action:** Awkward introductions. Eli stands up, explains he's Kat's brother, June asked him to help with Captain. Nora explains she's June's neighbor-slash-friend, also asked to help with Captain.
- **They both realize** June has double-booked them. Or, more accurately, June has set up redundant systems, because June was a teacher and teachers always have a backup plan.
- **Eli offers her coffee.** In June's house. With June's coffee. Nora accepts because she needs caffeine before she can figure out whether this is annoying or practical.
- **Dynamic:** Nora is friendly but assessing. She asks practical questions — does he have experience with dogs? (No.) Large dogs? (He had a beagle growing up. A beagle.) Does he know Captain's routine? (He just got here.) Nora is unconsciously ticking through a competency checklist, which is what she does. She doesn't realize it reads as slightly condescending.
- **Eli notices.** He's not offended — he's amused. He can see exactly what she's doing because he spent a decade in advertising reading rooms. He answers her questions honestly. No, he doesn't know what he's doing with the dog. He's here because his sister told him to leave the house.
- **Beat:** Nora is thrown by the honesty. Most people perform competence. This guy is openly admitting he's a dog amateur. It's either refreshing or concerning. She hasn't decided.

### Scene 3: The Negotiation (≈500 words)
- **Action:** They sit at June's kitchen table (Nora with coffee now, Captain lying across both their feet under the table) and work out logistics. Nora works weekdays, 8-5. She can do mornings and evenings. Captain can't be alone for more than twenty minutes without a meltdown. Someone needs to be with him during the day.
- **Eli, slowly:** "I work from home. I can be here during the day." (The "work from home" is technically true in the sense that he sits at home and stares at a blank document. He does not clarify.)
- **Nora proposes a schedule.** She'll come by in the mornings before work to feed Captain and walk him. Eli takes over from 8 to 5. She comes back after work for the evening shift. Weekends they can figure out as they go.
- **They exchange numbers.** Nora adds him to her phone as "Eli (Captain)." Eli notices this and says nothing.
- **Character beats:** The negotiation itself is the character work. Nora is organized, decisive, slightly over-prepared (she's already pulling up Captain's vet records on her phone to share dietary info). Eli is agreeable but low-energy — not lazy, just... conserving. Like someone who's learned to move carefully so they don't knock into the thing that hurts.
- **Moment of friction:** Nora starts explaining how to handle Captain's anxiety — the signs, the de-escalation, the things to avoid (toaster, plastic bags, the UPS truck). It's thorough. It's kind. It's also a lot. Eli listens, then says: "You're very good at making sure everyone knows exactly what to do." It's not mean. But Nora hears it. Her ex used to say something similar, and it didn't mean *you're competent*. It meant *you're controlling*. She doesn't say this. She just says, "Captain's sensitive. I want to make sure he's okay." Eli: "He's lucky to have you." And that one's genuine, and Nora doesn't know what to do with the whiplash.

### Scene 4: The Handoff (≈350 words)
- **Action:** Nora has to leave — she's off work today but needs to go home, shower, check in with Diane, deal with life. She walks Eli through the immediate stuff: where the food is, Captain's leash, the yard, the squeaky taco (do not lose the squeaky taco). Captain watches her put on her shoes with visible concern.
- **The moment:** Nora kneels down to say goodbye to Captain. He presses his face into her neck. She talks to him quietly — "You're okay, big guy. I'll be back tonight. Eli's going to hang out with you." She looks up at Eli. "He'll probably test you. He likes to see if new people will stay when he gets scared."
- **Eli, quietly:** "Don't we all."
- **Nora blinks.** Wasn't expecting that. She stands up, says something normal about texting her if anything comes up, and leaves.
- **Closing beat:** Walking to her car, Nora thinks about the man in June's kitchen. He's not what she expected when she heard "Kat's brother" — Kat is brisk and bright and high-wattage. Eli is... muted. Not unhappy, exactly. Just turned down. Like someone lowered his brightness setting.
- **Last line territory:** Something like: "I told him Captain tests people to see if they'll stay. He said, 'Don't we all,' like it was obvious. Like staying was the whole question. Maybe it is. I don't know him. I don't need to know him. I just need him to not lose the squeaky taco."
